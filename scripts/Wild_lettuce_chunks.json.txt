"chunk_text": "Wild lettuce (Lactuca virosa L.) is a tall biennial herb of the Asteraceae (daisy) family, closely related to cultivated lettuce. In its first year it forms a low rosette of deeply lobed leaves; in the second, it shoots up a stout, hollow flower stalk that can exceed 2 m (6–7 ft) in height. Leaves are alternate and oblong, with wavy or toothed margins and sometimes prickly hairs along the midrib underside (similar to its cousin prickly lettuce, L. serriola). The plant often has a reddish or purplish tinge on stems and leaf veins. When flowering, one wild lettuce may simultaneously bear buds, blooming flowers, and windborne seeds – a testament to its fast, continuous reproduction. In winter, the flowering stalks die, and the next generation persists as ground-hugging green rosettes, waiting out the frost.\n\nThroughout its range, Lactuca virosa has gathered many names. In English it’s commonly called wild lettuce, bitter lettuce, or great lettuce, alluding to its more robust, acrid nature compared to garden lettuce. The nickname “opium lettuce” (or lettuce opium) reflects its historical use as a pain-relieving sedative; 19th-century apothecaries dried its sap into a brown resin called lactucarium as a legal opiate substitute.
