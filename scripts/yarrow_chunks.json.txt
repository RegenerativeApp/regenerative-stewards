Family: Asteraceae (Daisy family), which also includes chamomiles, sunflowers, and asters. Yarrow’s flower heads are actually composite inflorescences typical of this family. Synonyms: Over time, botanists have split and lumped yarrow into many names. North American variants were once called Achillea lanulosa (western yarrow), but modern botany usually treats them as A. millefolium. Folk names like Milfoil (French mille-feuille), Nosebleed plant, and Carpenter’s weed also appear in old herbals. Native range: Achillea millefolium has a circumboreal distribution – native to temperate regions of Eurasia and North America. It grows wild from Europe’s meadows to Siberia’s steppes, from Arctic Alaska to northern Mexico’s highlands. Introduced range: Early colonists carried yarrow to the southern hemisphere; it’s naturalized in New Zealand and Australia (initially as a pasture feed). Today it’s found on every continent except Antarctica. In some areas it’s considered an aggressive weed – yarrow’s hardy rhizomes and prolific seeds let it invade lawns, field edges, and overgrazed pastures. However, it usually isn’t classed as a noxious invasive since it rarely dominates intact native ecosystems. It is a plentiful wild plant, assessed as Least Concern for conservation (not endangered). "chunk_text": "Look-alikes & Misidentification Hazards: Yarrow’s delicate, dissected leaves and flat white flower clusters can superficially resemble a few other plants – some benign, some deadly – so proper identification is important. The most notorious look-alike is Poison Hemlock (Conium maculatum), a highly toxic weed. Both have white blooms and finely divided leaves, but hemlock is in the carrot family (Umbelliferae) and can be distinguished by its taller, hollow green stems with distinctive purple blotches and a lack of yarrow’s woolly hairs or strong aroma. Yarrow, by contrast, has solid, green (sometimes reddish-tinged) stems that are not blotched, and when you crush its leaves they have a spicy medicinal smell (whereas poison hemlock has a foul, musty odor). Also, hemlock’s flowers form umbrella-shaped umbels with many thin spokes, while yarrow’s inflorescence is a flat-topped corymb of composite mini “daisies.” Another look-alike is Queen Anne’s Lace (wild carrot, Daucus carota), which has ferny leaves and white flat clusters, but again, Queen Anne’s lace has bristly stems and often a single dark purple floret in the center of its umbel. It’s also harmless (and edible) – so confusing yarrow with Queen Anne’s Lace is not dangerous, just a case of mistaken identity. Wild chamomile (Matricaria species) or pineapple weed can have somewhat similar feathery leaves, but their flowers differ. White Snakeroot (Ageratina altissima), a toxic Asteraceae plant, has white flower clusters at the same time of year – but its leaves are broad and opposite, nothing like yarrow’s fine alternates. Bottom line: true yarrow can be identified by its millefoliate (thousand-cut) leaves and its composite flower heads with 5–8 tiny ray florets each. When in doubt, use multiple cues: check the leaf structure and arrangement, the presence of any purple stem mottling (bad sign), and even do a small smell test – no look-alike shares yarrow’s distinctive camphor-like scent. Misidentifying yarrow with poison hemlock or other toxic Apiaceae could be fatal; foragers should always double-check these features and consult guidebooks. Fortunately, with practice the differences become clear, and yarrow has no truly poisonous twins in the daisy family."
